Hedera

Licence-free wireless audio for moving sound systems.

Hedera is a licence-free wireless audio + data link designed to connect moving carnival floats travelling along a parade route. It carries a minimum of one uncompressed stereo PCM audio stream with minimal (ideally imperceptible) latency, plus a service / messaging channel to keep engineers connected between floats.

Features Antenna & RF Timeline Roadmap

Built from field reality: vibration, obstructions, crowded streets, and saturated RF conditions.

Technical Overview

What Hedera delivers

  • Uncompressed audio: 16/24-bit PCM @ 48 kHz (minimum one stereo channel)
  • Low latency target: 3–5 ms AD/DA (deployment-tuned for stability)
  • Link resilience: packet redundancy and controlled buffering behaviour
  • Analogue I/O: dual XLR stereo line in/out
  • Digital option: stereo S/PDIF / AES input or output
  • Service channel: messaging / coordination data link between engineers

Where bandwidth allows, additional audio channels can be carried.

Antenna & RF

Why “donut” antennas fail on floats

Omnidirectional Wi-Fi antennas radiate in a donut pattern: wide horizontally, but very narrow vertically. On moving floats, vibration and road pitch can shift alignment and collapse the link.

Hedera is designed around controlled, symmetrical coverage so coverage remains predictable in motion.

  • Symmetrical beam pattern: 134°–180°
  • Azimuth beam width (per antenna): H 67°–90° / V 67°–90°
  • Elevation beam width (per antenna): H 67°–90° / V 67°–90°
Real-World Proven

Notting Hill Carnival development timeline

  • 2014: concept born — client demand to link floats to play “as one”
  • 2015: first live tests — uncompressed IP audio proven viable in motion
  • 2016: first proper run — antenna stability strong, commercial encoders introduced latency drift
  • 2017: shootout — compared against 1W RF and Neutrik Xirium; reliability validated
  • 2018: stable multi-day operation — latency reduced, resilience refined
  • 2019: extended separation tests — validated bridging node use in certain layouts

Key field outcomes included high link stability in hostile RF, and deployment learnings centred on geometry, vibration control, and predictable packet behaviour.
